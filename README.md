## Project Overview

This research uses Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) to simulate the US labor market and project how AI adoption will affect employment, wages, and workforce dynamics over a 5-year horizon. The simulation creates ~10,000 worker agents with realistic demographic and employment characteristics, then compares a control scenario (no AI displacement) against an experimental scenario (AI risk introduced).

---

## Research Questions

| # | Question | Hypothesis |
|---|----------|------------|
| 1 | How does AI adoption impact jobs and wages at macro/micro scales over 5 years? | Unemployment metrics should not deviate significantly between groups |
| 2 | What is the vulnerability gap between entry-level and senior workers? | Entry-level workers face significantly increased risk of layoff and longer job search periods |
| 3 | What impact does AI have on the wage gap between experience levels? | Wage gap will widen as AI augmentation benefits experienced workers more |
| 4 | Does AI de-value routine hard skills (programming, technical writing)? | Routine hard skills will be devalued as they are susceptible to LLM automation |
| 5 | Automation vs. Augmentation - which effect is stronger by experience level? | Experienced workers see augmentation; junior workers see automation |
| 6 | What job creation rate offsets AI displacement? | Ratio must be >1.0 due to frictional unemployment and skills mismatches |
| 7 | Is there an AI adoption tipping point causing industry labor demand collapse? | Collapse occurs at 40-50% AI tool saturation in high-exposure industries |
| 8 | Would government retraining subsidies offset negative impacts? | Yes, by reducing friction for workers transitioning to low-exposure fields |

## Data Validity Assessment

### The Core Validity Problem

This research relies on AI risk scores (automation probability, augmentation potential) to drive the experimental treatment in the ABM. **If these scores are primarily LLM-generated, the findings may reflect the LLM's training biases rather than economic reality.**

### Empirical vs. Synthetic Data Breakdown

| Component | Data Source | Type | Validity |
|-----------|-------------|------|----------|
| Worker demographics | IPUMS CPS | Empirical | High - actual survey responses |
| Employment status, wages | IPUMS CPS | Empirical | High - actual survey responses |
| Skill taxonomy | O*NET | Empirical | High - job analyst surveys |
| Skill importance/level by occupation | O*NET | Empirical | High - standardized ratings |
| Industry performance | BTOS | Empirical | High - business survey |
| AI adoption rates | BTOS | Empirical | Medium - self-reported |
| **AI Exposure scores (AIOE)** | Felten et al. (2021) | **Empirical** | **High - peer reviewed** |
| **Language Model Exposure (LM_AIOE)** | Felten et al. (2023) | **Empirical** | **High - peer reviewed** |
| **Skill distance matrix** | TBD (LLM or O*NET) | Synthetic/Empirical | Medium |

### Risk Assessment

**High Risk:** The primary treatment effect (AI risk -> layoff probability) is entirely driven by LLM-generated scores. If the simulation shows "programmers are more likely to be displaced," this finding may simply reflect that the LLM rated programming as high-risk, not any emergent property of the labor market simulation.

**Circular Reasoning Danger:** Asking an LLM trained on 2023-era discourse about AI automation to predict automation risk produces scores that reflect media narratives, not economic fundamentals.

### Mitigations to Strengthen Validity

#### Option A: Use Peer-Reviewed AI Exposure Indices (Recommended)
Instead of generating scores with an LLM, use published, peer-reviewed indices:

| Index | Source | Methodology | Coverage |
|-------|--------|-------------|----------|
| GPT Exposure Index | Eloundou et al. (2023) | Expert + GPT-4 ratings | ~800 occupations |
| AI Occupational Exposure | Webb (2020) | Patent-task matching | All O*NET occupations |
| AI Occupational Impact | Felten, Raj & Seamans (2021, 2023) | AI capability mapping | All O*NET occupations |
| Suitability for Machine Learning | Brynjolfsson, Mitchell & Rock (2018) | Task-level assessment | O*NET work activities |

**Recommendation:** Use Felten et al. (2023) or Webb (2020) as primary AI exposure scores. These have undergone peer review and can be validated against actual employment trends.

#### Option B: Composite Scoring Approach
If LLM scoring is retained, combine with empirical signals:

```
composite_risk = (
    0.3 * llm_automation_score +
    0.3 * routine_task_score_from_onet +
    0.2 * published_ai_exposure_index +
    0.2 * inverse_of_physical_proximity_requirement
)
```

#### Option C: Sensitivity Analysis (Required Either Way)
Run the simulation with multiple AI risk score sources:
1. LLM-generated scores (baseline)
2. Eloundou et al. GPT Exposure Index
3. Randomized scores (null model)
4. Inverted scores (robustness check)

If conclusions hold across all scoring methods, findings are robust. If conclusions change with different scores, the findings are score-dependent and less meaningful.

#### Option D: Ground Truth Validation
After generating LLM scores, validate against 2022-2025 employment data:
- Do occupations the LLM rates as "high risk" actually show employment decline?
- Compute correlation between LLM risk scores and actual employment trends
- Report this correlation as a validity metric

### Recommended Approach

1. **Primary scores:** Obtain Felten et al. (2023) AI Occupational Impact scores
2. **Secondary scores:** Generate LLM scores as originally planned
3. **Validation:** Compare LLM scores vs. published indices (report correlation)
4. **Sensitivity:** Run scenarios with both score sets
5. **Report:** Clearly state which findings are robust vs. score-dependent

### What This Means for Conclusions

With proper mitigations, the research can make meaningful claims about:
- **Robust findings:** Results that hold regardless of AI risk score source
- **Conditional findings:** Results that depend on specific assumptions about AI risk (clearly labeled)
- **Methodological contributions:** The ABM framework itself, which can be re-run as better AI exposure data becomes available

Without mitigations, findings would be limited to: "If we assume [LLM's view of AI risk], then [outcome]" - which is tautological.

## Technical Architecture

### Core Technologies
- **Language:** Python 3.12
- **ABM Framework:** Mesa
- **Data Processing:** Pandas, NumPy
- **Visualization:** Matplotlib, Seaborn
- **LLM Inference:** Ollama (local, recommended model: llama3:8b)
- **Ontology:** RDF/OWL (Turtle format)
- **Knowledge Graph (optional):** Mobi, Altair Graph Studio

### Ontology Structure
See [ontology/WorkerImpactOntology.ttl](ontology/WorkerImpactOntology.ttl) for the formal schema.

**Classes:**
- `Agent` (parent class)
  - `Worker` - simulated labor market participant
  - `Employer` - entity that posts jobs
- `Skill` - individual competency (subclass of SKOS Concept)
- `SkillCategory` - grouping of related skills (subclass of SKOS ConceptScheme)
- `Job` - position with required skills and salary range
- `Tick` - simulation time step (1 tick = 1 month)

**Key Properties:**
- `hasSkill` - Worker -> Skill
- `requiresSkill` - Job -> Skill
- `automationRisk` - Skill -> float (0-10 scale)
- `augmentationRisk` - Skill -> float (0-10 scale)
- `monthsExperience` - Skill -> int

#### Step 2.4: Tick Logic Implementation
Monthly simulation cycle:

```
For each tick (month):
  1. Job Search Phase
     - Unemployed agents search for jobs
     - Match probability = f(skills, experience, vacancies)

  2. Layoff Phase
     - Control: P(layoff) = f(industry_performance, agent_performance)
     - Experimental: P(layoff) += f(ai_risk, experience_discount)

  3. Skill Revaluation Phase (Experimental only)
     - Routine skills depreciate
     - Soft skills appreciate

  4. Experience Gain Phase
     - Employed agents: experience += 1

  5. Skill Mobility Phase
     - Dissatisfied agents consider retraining
     - Choice = f(vacancies, ai_risk, skill_distance)
```
